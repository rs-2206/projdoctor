5 Milestones in Public Healthcare that have changed our lives




Public health has had a good impact on the human race throughout history, and as early as 1848, it was essential in bringing sanitation, cleanliness, and mental health. The globe has witnessed one of the most extraordinary illnesses in recent history over the last year. COVID-19 changed all of our lives and had a significant impact on the way we work, do business, and interact with one another as a human race, but with the recent COVID-19 vaccine, our lives are well on their way to being completely transformed for the better, but the vaccine is just one of many outstanding public health breakthroughs that have forever changed our lives.
Here is a list of five incredible accomplishments in public healthcare.

  • Vaccines against COVID-19

    • When COVID-19 cases peaked in March 2020 throughout the world, the public health sector was left in a state of shock, disorder, and bewilderment. As the virus spread like wildfire over the world, fear ensued, leaving many governments unprepared and helpless, causing panic and sentiments of pessimism in the public health sector. In January 2020, scientists began working on the COVID-19 vaccine. Dedicated vaccine financing in the United States aided in the rapid and successful progression of vaccine candidates through pre-clinical and clinical assessments and trials. To save time, the vaccine was mass manufactured before the clinical investigations were completed. Pfizer, Moderna, and Johnson & Johnson developed and distributed the vaccine.

  • Maternal and Infant Health

    • Ending unnecessary maternal mortality is at the top of the global public health agenda, and the public health sector has made significant progress in this area in recent years. Addressing health inequities, particularly those affecting sexual and reproductive health and rights, is critical to ensuring that all women have access to respectful and high-quality maternity care. Over the last decade, the public health sector has made great strides in this regard.

  • Recognition of tobacco use as a health hazard

    • Tobacco use has been identified as a health threat, and the numerous illnesses related with tobacco use have been identified by public health. Tobacco use is also linked to heart disease, atherosclerotic peripheral vascular disease, laryngeal cancer, oral cancer, esophageal cancer, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease, intrauterine development retardation, and low birth weight.

  • Prevention and Control of HIV/AIDS

    • Acquired immunodeficiency syndrome (AIDS) is a potentially fatal illness caused by the human immunodeficiency virus (HIV). HIV impairs your body's capacity to fight infection and illness by damaging your immune system. HIV is a sexually transmitted infection (STD). The illness can also be passed from mother to child during pregnancy, delivery, or nursing if there is direct contact with contaminated blood. Without medicine, it might take years for HIV to weaken your immune system to the point where you develop AIDS. There is presently no cure for HIV/AIDS, however treatment can significantly delay the disease's course. In many industrialised countries, public health has enabled these medications to minimise AIDS fatalities.

  • HIPAA

    • The Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act, or HIPAA, was approved by Congress in 1996, and it protects a patient's medical information from being revealed without the patient's permission. Although HIPAA has tight administrative and technological restrictions, it is best recognised for protecting health information privacy. This provision of the law has shielded patients from insurance discrimination, making it simpler for them to obtain and keep their health insurance.

    All of these public health breakthroughs have irrevocably altered the trajectory of human history.
