Benefits of Investing in Today's Medical Startups




Investing in health-related start-ups is popular. Doctors are now joining the fray. At least two organisations, FundRx in New York and angelMD in Seattle, are bringing together physicians with extra cash and medical companies in need of investment. AngelMD's proposition to businesses is to close a round with capital from "people who know healthcare the best." Do physicians truly have the greatest advice when it comes to investing new businesses? Depending on who you ask.

Physicians having experience in investing or running a business are well-suited to evaluate medical start-ups. Perhaps even better than technology investors, who may be unfamiliar with the complexity and nuances of the health business. FundRx, for example, is sponsored by two physicians with business experience: Eric Golding, a former hedge fund executive, and Aron Ron, a former operating partner at venture firm Bessemer Venture Partners. "At the end of the day," says Zeshan Muhammedi, co-founder of the fund, "you want the end user involved because it's their problem you're trying to address."

AngelMD provides business and finance training to doctors who have never worked in industry but wish to invest. It's unclear if practitioners can be trained to be good angel investors; it may go either way. However, it is doubtful that physicians would invest in promising new technology that would undermine some component of their employment or transfer over control to a nurse or a patient. Nonetheless, many early-stage companies actively seek financing from doctors.

According to Halle Tecco, co-founder of the venture company Rock Health, technological start-ups in other sectors frequently receive angel financing from senior leaders in their industry who have already made their money through exits. However, with so few successful digital health acquisitions and IPOs, money from health providers may fill the need.

And, as Tecco stated, physicians care deeply about human health and are well-versed in the issues. Start-ups provide a possible source of revenue (although a hazardous one) for doctors, as well as an opportunity to disrupt the status quo in medicine.

"Doctors want to develop and create, but their day jobs don't allow it," said Choy, who prefers to advise start-ups in return for stock rather than invest directly. But he isn't dismissing the possibility. Because fewer physicians are operating their own offices these days, med-tech is also an entrepreneurial outlet for them. "Entrepreneurship in medicine is more difficult to pursue than before," Choy remarked.
